Representativeness Heuristic


In the case of insensitivity to sample size, people assume local representativeness in that small samples should be representative of the population, and thus think that long streaks of H or T in a small sample is representative of a non-fair coin. For the gambler's fallacy, people believe the process to be random, and try to impose that belief on what they observe. When what they observe is not representative of their belief of randomness, they believe that it has now become more likely that the process will somehow revert to something that looks more random by breaking the streak. For the hot hand fallacy, people do not believe the process to be random, and impose that belief on their predictions that players who have made several shots in a row are more likely to make their next shot, even when empirical evidence shows otherwise.

So what does this all mean?


In order to help us save time and energy, evolution gave us the ability to use heuristics as shortcuts to make quick judgments about the world around us. While they work well most of the time, the use of heuristics can sometimes lead to incorrect judgments. Both the Representativeness and the Availability Heuristics are sources of cognitive biases when humans make judgments on frequency and probability, meaning that trusting our gut, or our instincts, is not always the wisest decision.
